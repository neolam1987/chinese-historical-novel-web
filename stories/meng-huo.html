Capturing Meng Huo… Seven Times!

七擒孟獲
🐘 War elephants! ⏱️ ~8 min read 🎙️ Sports commentary

🎭 Who's in this story?

Zhuge Liang (諸葛亮)Strategist on an away game Deep in the southern jungles, far from home.
Meng Huo (孟獲)King of the southern tribes Strong as a buffalo, brave as a tiger, stubborn as… well, you'll see.
Lady Zhurong (祝融夫人)Meng Huo's wife A fearsome warrior queen who throws flying daggers. Cooler than everyone.
King Mulu (木鹿大王)Meng Huo's ally Commands tigers, leopards and war elephants. Basically a jungle wizard.

GOOOOOD MORNING, sports fans, and welcome to the deep, steamy jungles of Nanzhong (南中)!1 I'm your commentator high up in the banyan-tree broadcast booth, and folks, you have tuned in to the strangest tournament in military history! In the away corner: the legendary strategist ZHUGE LIANG (諸葛亮), with his feather fan and his clever, clever brain. In the home corner: the undefeated king of the south, the mountain of muscle, MENG HUO (孟獲)! The prize? The loyalty of the entire southern lands. And folks — this one goes to SEVEN ROUNDS.

But first, a word about the rules, because they are bizarre. Before the tournament, Zhuge Liang's advisor Ma Su (馬謖) gave him this advice: "Conquering cities is easy. Conquering hearts is the real victory. Beat them with fear, and they'll rebel the moment you leave."2 So Zhuge Liang set himself a secret rule: Meng Huo must not just be defeated — he must ADMIT defeat, from the heart. Let's see how that goes!

🎙️ Round 1 — The Head-On Charge

Meng Huo charges with his full army — brave, loud, zero planning! Zhuge Liang fakes a retreat, Meng Huo chases at full speed and runs straight into a valley ambush! Captured! And here's the twist, folks: Zhuge Liang gives him dinner and asks, "Do you admit defeat?" Meng Huo snorts: "You tricked me! That doesn't count! Rematch!" And Zhuge Liang — I can't believe what I'm seeing — unties him and lets him walk home! SCORE: 1–0.

The crowd in Zhuge Liang's own camp is stunned, folks. His officers protest: "We had him! Why let him go?!" And the strategist just smiles behind that feather fan: "Catch him once, I win a battle. Convince him, I win the south — forever. Patience, gentlemen. We're playing the long game."

🎙️ Round 2 — The Midnight River Sneak

Meng Huo learns from Round 1 — no more open charges! He camps behind the Lu River (瀘水), planning to wait out the summer heat. But his own officers, tired of his temper, tie him up in his sleep and deliver him to Zhuge Liang like a takeaway order! "THIS DOESN'T COUNT EITHER!" bellows Meng Huo. "My own men betrayed me — you didn't beat ME!" Fair point, actually, folks. Released again! SCORE: 2–0.

🎙️ Round 3 — The Brother Trick

Sneaky play from the home team! Meng Huo sends his brother to Zhuge Liang's camp pretending to surrender, with a hundred "gift-bearers" — actually hidden warriors, folks, a classic fake-gift gambit! But Zhuge Liang smells it instantly, serves the "guests" extra-strong wine, and when Meng Huo sneaks in that night for his surprise attack, the surprise is on HIM — his men are asleep and the camp is a trap! Captured! "I ate too much dinner! Doesn't count!" RELEASED! SCORE: 3–0.

Folks, I've been commentating battles for thirty years and I have NEVER seen a scoreboard like this. Between rounds, let me tell you what the strategist is up to. Every time Meng Huo walks free, Zhuge Liang's soldiers escort him politely, feed him, even give him gifts for the road. The southern tribespeople are watching all of it. Whispers are spreading through the villages: "This northern strategist… he doesn't burn our homes. He keeps releasing our king. What kind of enemy IS this?" That, sports fans, is the real game — and it's being won between the rounds.

🎙️ Rounds 4 & 5 — Enter the Warrior Queen

Round 4: Meng Huo tries a poisoned-spring gauntlet — luring Zhuge Liang's army through valleys with toxic waters! The strategist finds a wise hermit who reveals the antidote springs. Captured again — "The water didn't count!" — released again! Round 5 brings the tournament's superstar: LADY ZHURONG (祝融夫人), Meng Huo's wife, thundering in on horseback with her flying daggers! She captures TWO of Zhuge Liang's generals — the away team's first real losses, folks! But Zhuge Liang lures her into an ambush with a fake retreat and trades prisoners politely. Meng Huo? Captured in the confusion. AGAIN. "DOESN'T COUNT!" SCORE: 5–0.

🎙️ Round 6 — THE WAR ELEPHANTS 🐘

Folks, hold onto your hats — Meng Huo calls in his ally King Mulu (木鹿大王), a jungle sorcerer riding a giant white elephant, commanding TIGERS, LEOPARDS, and a full stampede of WAR ELEPHANTS! Zhuge Liang's horses panic! The away team retreats in chaos! Is this the comeback?! …It is NOT, sports fans, because Zhuge Liang rolls out his secret weapon: giant WOODEN BEAST-MACHINES, painted in wild colours, breathing smoke from hidden firepots!3 The real animals take one look at these smoke-snorting monsters, turn around, and stampede over their own army! Meng Huo: captured. Number six. "The elephants panicked! DOESN'T COUNT!" SCORE: 6–0.

🎙️ Round 7 — The Final

And so we come to the last round, folks. Meng Huo has one card left: he borrows the fearsome rattan-armour warriors of King Wutugu (兀突骨) — soldiers wearing armour of woven vines soaked in oil, so tough that arrows and swords bounce right off! Unstoppable! Except… oil-soaked armour has one tiny weakness. Can you guess it, sports fans? That's right: it burns. Zhuge Liang lures the rattan army into a narrow valley and unleashes a firestorm. It's a total victory — but up in my tree, I can see the strategist's face, and he is not celebrating. He's weeping. "This victory will cost me years of my life," he says quietly. Even winners, folks, can hate what winning costs.4

Meng Huo is captured for the seventh time. Seven! The crowd holds its breath as he's brought forward. Zhuge Liang doesn't even meet him this time — he just sends a messenger with a shrug: "The Prime Minister is embarrassed to see you again. He says: go home, gather another army, and come back for round eight if you like."

And Meng Huo… doesn't move. The big man's shoulders start to shake. Then the king of the south goes down on his knees, tears rolling into his beard, and cries out the words the whole jungle has waited for:

"Seven times captured, seven times released! Such a thing has never happened in all the world. I may be a wild man, but even I know what honour is. I will never rebel again — the south follows you, from the HEART!"

WHAT A FINISH, LADIES AND GENTLEMEN! Meng Huo surrenders — not to fear, but to kindness! And here's the final masterstroke: Zhuge Liang doesn't leave a single occupying soldier behind. No northern governors, no garrisons. He puts Meng Huo himself in charge of the south and marches home. His officers are baffled one last time, and he explains: "Leave soldiers, and I must feed them. Leave strangers ruling, and the people will resent them. Leave a friend in charge — and I never need to come back." And folks, he never did. The south stayed loyal for as long as Zhuge Liang lived.

✦ ✦ ✦

FINAL SCORE: Captures 7, Grudges 0. What a tournament, sports fans. Remember this one next time you beat your little cousin at a board game seven times in a row: winning the game is easy — winning the player takes patience, respect, and maybe letting them believe in round eight. From the banyan-tree booth, GOOD NIGHT! 🎙️🐘

📜 History Corner (footnotes)

  1. Nanzhong (南中) covered today's Yunnan, Guizhou and southern Sichuan — hot, mountainous jungle country in China's far southwest. Its tribes had rebelled against Shu (蜀) in 225 CE, and Zhuge Liang personally led the campaign to win the region back. ↩
  2. "Attack hearts first, attack cities second" (攻心為上，攻城為下) became one of the most famous strategy quotes in Chinese history. People still use it today — in business, in sport, even in making up after an argument! ↩
  3. Zhuge Liang was famous as an inventor. Stories credit him with the repeating crossbow (諸葛弩), the "wooden ox" transport cart (木牛流馬), and the sky lantern (孔明燈) — which is named after him and still lights up festivals today. ↩
  4. The novel makes a point of showing that Zhuge Liang took no joy in destroying the rattan-armour army. Great strategists in Chinese stories are supposed to feel the weight of war, not celebrate it — victory without mercy was considered no victory at all. ↩
