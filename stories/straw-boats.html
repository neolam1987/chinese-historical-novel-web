Borrowing Arrows with Straw Boats

草船借箭
🧠 Brains beat muscle ⏱️ ~8 min read 📰 News report style

🎭 Who's in this story?

Zhuge Liang (諸葛亮)The genius strategist Can read the weather like you read a comic book.
Zhou Yu (周瑜)Wu's top commander Brilliant — but burning with jealousy.
Lu Su (魯肅)The kind messenger Honest, gentle, and permanently worried.
Cao Cao (曹操)The enemy warlord Commands 800,000 soldiers across the river.

📰 THE RIVER TIMES — Special Report

Good morning, readers! I'm your reporter on the banks of the great Yangtze River (長江), where the allied camps of Wu (吳) and Shu (蜀) are preparing for war against the mighty warlord Cao Cao (曹操).1 And I have just witnessed the most unbelievable three days in military history. Grab your breakfast — you are not going to believe this one.

⚡ Breaking News — Day One

Commander Zhou Yu (周瑜) has ordered the visiting strategist Zhuge Liang (諸葛亮) to produce 100,000 arrows in just ten days. Zhuge Liang shocked the entire war council by replying: "Ten days? Too slow. I'll do it in three." He even signed a military pledge — if he fails, he accepts the punishment. Which, in an army camp, is very, very serious indeed.

Now, dear readers, let me explain what's really going on here, because this is juicier than a dumpling. Zhou Yu is the dashing, talented commander of the Wu forces. But sources inside the camp whisper that he is wildly jealous of Zhuge Liang, the strategist sent by Liu Bei (劉備) to help in this war. Every time Zhou Yu comes up with a clever plan, Zhuge Liang has already thought of it — sometimes days earlier. Imagine always coming second in class to the new kid. That's Zhou Yu's life right now.

So this arrow challenge? It was never a real request. Our camp insiders confirm it was a trap. Making 100,000 arrows requires wood, feathers, glue, and hundreds of craftsmen working for weeks. Zhou Yu even secretly ordered the workshop to work slowly. He wanted Zhuge Liang to fail, so he could punish him and be rid of his rival forever.

And Zhuge Liang just… smiled, fanned himself with his famous feather fan, and went off to have tea.

✦ ✦ ✦

Day One: The strategist does absolutely nothing

This reporter followed Zhuge Liang all day. Did he gather wood? No. Did he hire craftsmen? No. He drank tea, read books, and took what witnesses describe as "a really excellent nap." The camp is baffled. Zhou Yu is reportedly delighted.

There was one strange event. Zhuge Liang met privately with Lu Su (魯肅), the kindest and most honest officer in the Wu camp, and made an unusual request: twenty boats, each with thirty sailors, stacks of straw bundled into the shape of soldiers, and big cloth covers. "And please," Zhuge Liang added with a wink, "don't tell Zhou Yu. If he finds out, my plan is finished."

Lu Su, bless him, was completely confused. Boats? Straw men? To make arrows?! But he liked Zhuge Liang, so he agreed and kept the secret. He told Zhou Yu only that Zhuge Liang wasn't using any bamboo, feathers or glue. Zhou Yu frowned and said, "Then let's see what he does on day three."

Day Two: Still nothing!

More tea. Another nap. This reporter is beginning to worry about the man. One camp cook was overheard saying, "He does know arrows don't grow on boats, right?" The deadline is tomorrow. The workshop has produced exactly zero arrows for him. The pledge he signed still stands.

✦ ✦ ✦

Day Three: The 4 a.m. mystery cruise

Before sunrise on the third day, Zhuge Liang knocked on Lu Su's door. "Come along, my friend. We're going to collect the arrows." Lu Su rubbed his eyes. "Collect them? From where?" Zhuge Liang just smiled. "You'll see."

And here, readers, is where I must describe the weather, because the weather is the hero of this story. The river had vanished under the thickest fog anyone could remember.2 You could not see your own hand in front of your face. It was the kind of fog that swallows sounds and ships whole.

The twenty boats — straw soldiers standing in neat rows on the decks — slipped silently across the river, straight toward Cao Cao's enormous naval base. Lu Su nearly fainted. "We have no weapons! If they attack, we're finished!" Zhuge Liang poured him a cup of tea and said the most famous line of the whole affair:

"Relax, my friend. Cao Cao is a careful man. In fog this thick, he'll never risk sending his ships out — he can't see if it's a trap. He'll do exactly one thing. Watch. Also, drink your tea before it gets cold."

Then Zhuge Liang gave the strangest battle order in history: the sailors began beating war drums and screaming battle cries at the top of their lungs. DONG! DONG! DONG! "ATTAAACK!" they hollered — while their boats did absolutely nothing at all.

Inside the enemy camp

Our sources later confirmed what happened on Cao Cao's side, and it went exactly — exactly — as Zhuge Liang predicted. Cao Cao heard war drums thundering out of a wall of fog. He rushed out and saw the dim shapes of boats packed with soldiers. Was it the whole enemy navy? A trap? Impossible to tell!

"Do not sail out!" Cao Cao commanded. "In this fog they could ambush us! Archers — to the shore! Fire everything! Push them back with arrows!"

And so ten thousand archers lined the riverbank and fired. Arrows fell like rain, like hail, like the world's most dangerous fireworks show — and every single one of them thunked harmlessly into the straw soldiers. The straw men bristled like porcupines. When one side of the boats was completely full, Zhuge Liang calmly ordered the fleet to turn around so the arrows could fill up the other side. Readers, he even balanced the boats. The man thinks of everything.

As the sun began to burn away the fog, the fleet slipped back toward home. Zhuge Liang had the sailors shout one last message across the water: "Thank you for the arrows, Prime Minister Cao!"3 Cao Cao's face, according to one fisherman who saw it, "turned the colour of a ripe plum." By the time his ships gave chase, the straw boats were long gone.

✦ ✦ ✦
⚡ Final Count

Soldiers on the dock pulled the arrows from the straw and counted: over 100,000 arrows, delivered before the deadline. Cost to the Wu army: zero. Arrows made: zero. Arrows borrowed: all of them.

When Lu Su told Zhou Yu the whole story, the commander went quiet for a long time. Then he sighed the sigh of a man who has just lost a game he invented himself: "He knew the fog would come. He knew what Cao Cao would do before Cao Cao knew. I cannot compete with this man." For once, Zhou Yu's jealousy took a day off — replaced by pure amazement.

How did Zhuge Liang know? He explained it simply: a good strategist studies everything — the sky, the river, and most of all, people. He had watched the weather patterns for days and knew thick fog was coming. And he knew Cao Cao's character: clever but cautious, and far too suspicious to charge into a fog. "Knowing the weather is science," he said, tapping his feather fan. "Knowing people — that is the real weapon."

This has been The River Times. Remember, dear readers: when someone hands you an impossible problem, don't panic. Have a cup of tea, look at the sky, and think. Somewhere out there, there may be 100,000 arrows with your name on them. 🏹

📜 History Corner (footnotes)

  1. This story happens just before the Battle of Red Cliffs (赤壁之戰), around the year 208 CE. The two smaller kingdoms, Wu (吳) and Shu (蜀), teamed up because Cao Cao (曹操) was coming south with a gigantic army to conquer them both. Alone, each would lose — together, they had a chance. ↩
  2. The Yangtze River (長江) is the longest river in Asia — and in winter, warm river water meeting cold air creates famously thick fog. Zhuge Liang had studied the local weather carefully, so this "lucky" fog wasn't luck at all. ↩
  3. "Prime Minister" (丞相) was Cao Cao's official title — he ruled in the emperor's name. Thanking him politely for accidentally donating 100,000 arrows made the prank even cheekier. ↩
